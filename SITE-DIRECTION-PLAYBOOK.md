# joearmani.com — Site Direction Playbook

> Working strategy document for evolving joearmani.com into a repeat-visit resource for software professionals.
>
> Kept in the public repo intentionally so the site's direction is versioned alongside the code. This document covers direction, not private planning or implementation details.
>
> It complements [WEBSITE-BUILD.md](./WEBSITE-BUILD.md), which captures the original build specification and deployment context.

---

## 1. Why This Exists

The site has launched. The next problem is usefulness.

The launch version proves:

- the site is live
- the site looks credible
- the technical foundation is stable
- there is a place to publish

What it does **not** yet prove is that people have a reason to return regularly.

That is the next job.

The site should evolve from:

- a personal website with writing and project pages

into:

- a trusted resource for engineers who want practical guidance on agentic development, applied AI, and production-minded building

The goal is not simply to look impressive. The goal is to become useful often enough that people build a habit around checking the site, subscribing to the newsletter, and sharing specific pages.

---

## 2. Core Thesis

This site should win on **practicality**, **specificity**, and **earned perspective**.

There are many sites that cover:

- AI news
- coding tool hype
- generic software career advice
- vague founder storytelling

There are fewer sites that combine all of these traits:

- real software engineering experience
- current hands-on use of AI coding tools
- active product building
- willingness to document what failed
- operational taste about what survives contact with production

That combination is the advantage.

The site should therefore make a very clear promise:

> If you are building software with AI agents and want practical, production-minded guidance instead of hype, this site will save you time and help you make better decisions.

---

## 3. The Job of the Site

The site has four jobs.

### Job 1: Build trust

The site should quickly establish that Joe is not theorizing from the sidelines.

Signals that matter:

- active production engineering background
- current work on agentic systems
- real shipped products
- concrete artifacts and examples
- honest discussion of tradeoffs and failures

### Job 2: Create repeat utility

The site should give readers something worth revisiting:

- updated guides
- recurring field notes
- fresh experiments
- reusable templates
- clear recommendations

### Job 3: Turn attention into returning readers

The site should turn readers into:

- newsletter subscribers
- repeat visitors
- professional conversations
- collaborators

### Job 4: Accumulate long-term leverage

Each strong page should do more than publish a thought. It should become an asset that keeps working:

- a guide that ranks
- a page that gets shared
- a checklist people bookmark
- a template people copy
- a post that becomes the canonical answer to a recurring question

---

## 4. The Audience

The site should optimize for a clear primary audience and serve others secondarily.

### Primary Audience

Software engineers, staff-level developers, technical leads, and serious builders who:

- are experimenting with AI coding tools
- want better workflows, not demos
- care about production reliability
- want to understand what actually works

### Secondary Audience

Potential employers, founders, and technical collaborators who want to evaluate Joe's judgment, technical level, and communication quality.

### Tertiary Audience

Ambitious developers or AI-curious professionals who want a practical view into modern engineering workflows.

### What These Readers Actually Want

They do not mainly want biography.

They want:

- better decisions
- fewer mistakes
- reusable patterns
- stronger mental models
- faster adoption of tools that actually matter

The site should treat those outcomes as the product.

---

## 5. Positioning

### Positioning Statement

Joe Armani is a senior software engineer and builder documenting how AI-augmented and agentic development actually works in practice, with an emphasis on real workflows, production constraints, and reusable systems.

### What Makes This Positioning Strong

- It is rooted in active work.
- It is specific to a live technical shift.
- It creates a bridge between personal credibility and audience utility.
- It gives the site a focused subject area without making it one-dimensional.

### What the Site Should Not Become

Do not let the site drift into:

- generic AI commentary
- broad motivational writing
- trend summaries with no firsthand experience
- generic career-story content with no practical takeaway
- a static portfolio that rarely changes

Those formats may generate occasional attention, but they do not create a strong repeat-visit habit.

---

## 6. Strategic Promise to Readers

The site should consistently deliver one or more of these outcomes:

- save the reader time
- reduce the reader's risk
- help the reader make a decision
- give the reader something reusable

If a page does not do one of those things, it is probably low-value content.

### The Real Value Model

The site is most valuable when it publishes:

- frameworks
- checklists
- templates
- comparisons
- postmortems
- implementation notes
- operating principles

These are the formats that professionals bookmark and return to.

- repo setup for AI coding tools
- review loops
- verification workflows

- prompt structure

This is the pillar that makes the site feel alive.

Topics:

- what changed this week
- what broke
- what got better
- what assumptions failed
- what tools held up or did not

Why it matters:

- people return for current signal
- it creates an ongoing narrative
- it provides a steady publishing cadence without requiring a massive post every time

This pillar broadens the site's utility beyond AI-specific readers.

Topics:

- quality control
- code review
- validation
- deployment hygiene
- workflow design
- practical architecture

Why it matters:

- it anchors the site in serious engineering
- it prevents the site from becoming AI-hype-only
- it attracts readers who care about software quality

This pillar supports credibility and depth.

Topics:

- RL trading work
- practical ML experiments
- product rebuild decisions
- lessons from current projects

Why it matters:

- it demonstrates range
- it keeps the site tied to real building
- it creates stronger project-to-writing loops

This should remain a supporting pillar, not the center of gravity.

Topics:

- the pivot from woodshop to code

Why it matters:

- it is useful for trust, but not enough by itself to create repeat visits

## 8. The Content Types That Create Return Visits

Not all posts are equal. Some get read once. Some become habits.

The site should prioritize these content types.

### Type 1: Evergreen Guides

Purpose:

- become canonical resources
- rank in search
- attract links
- earn bookmarks

Examples:

- How I structure an AI-friendly repo without losing control
- A practical review workflow for agent-generated code
- What to automate, what to verify, and what to keep manual

### Type 2: Recurring Series

Purpose:

- create anticipation
- establish a recognizable format
- lower the cost of regular publishing

Examples:

- What Broke This Week in Agentic Development
- One Workflow Worth Stealing
- Tooling Notes from the Front Line
- Codex vs Claude vs Gemini on the Same Task

### Type 3: Postmortems and Failure Reports

Purpose:

- build trust
- teach through mistakes
- provide differentiated signal

Examples:

- A deployment issue and how it was diagnosed
- A workflow that looked good but collapsed under scale
- A model/tool choice that created hidden costs

### Type 4: Reusable Assets

Purpose:

- make the site tangible
- increase shares and subscriptions
- create strong reasons to return

Examples:

- AGENTS.md starter
- repo review checklist
- task spec template
- bug reproduction checklist
- build verification checklist

### Type 5: Ongoing Build Narratives

Purpose:

- make the site feel active
- connect projects to lessons
- create a “follow along” dynamic

Examples:

- rebuilding Racquet Rivalry with agentic workflows
- evolving a multi-agent development system
- lessons from RL experimentation

## 9. Proposed Site Architecture

The current site structure is solid, but it should evolve from “pages about me and what I do” into “pages that help visitors do better work.”

### Keep

- Home
- Blog
- Projects
- Uses
- Now
- About
- Subscribe

### Add

- Resources
- Agentic Development Field Guide
- Experiments or Lab Notes

### Recommended Navigation Direction

- Blog
- Resources
- Projects
- Uses
- About
- Subscribe

`Now` can stay linked from the footer or About unless it becomes a bigger surface.

### Why Add a Resources Page

This becomes the highest-value hub on the site.

It should aggregate:

- templates
- checklists
- starter files
- decision frameworks
- best posts by topic

This is the kind of page professionals bookmark and revisit.

### Why Add a Field Guide

The site needs one flagship resource that clearly answers:

> What is Joe’s best, most complete thinking on agentic development right now?

That page should be updated over time and linked from the home page, blog posts, and newsletter.

## 10. Homepage Rearchitecture

The homepage should stop behaving like a simple introduction and start behaving like a high-signal front door.

### Primary Homepage Goal

Help a first-time visitor understand:

- what this site is for
- why it is worth trusting
- where to start
- what to come back for

### Recommended Homepage Blocks

1. Hero

The hero should clearly state:

- who Joe is
- what the site helps with
- why the perspective is credible

2. Start Here

A curated block with the best 3 to 5 pages for new readers:

- flagship guide
- best personal story
- strongest current workflow post
- most useful template/resource

3. Latest Field Notes

Short, recent posts that make the site feel active.

4. Resources

Highlight templates, checklists, and reusable guides.

5. What I’m Building

Keep this, but frame it as evidence and context rather than the main event.

6. Newsletter CTA

The CTA should promise practical value, not generic updates.

### Homepage Rule

Every homepage section should answer one of these:

- where do I start?
- what can I use right now?
- what is new here?
- why should I trust this person?

## 11. Blog Strategy

The blog should not be a catch-all. It should become a structured publishing system.

### Recommended Post Categories

- Guides
- Field Notes
- Postmortems
- Comparisons
- Build Logs

### Recommended Publishing Mix

- short tactical post every 1 to 2 weeks
- deeper guide every 3 to 5 weeks
- recurring series entry on a predictable cadence

### Every Strong Post Should Include

- the problem
- the real-world context
- what was tried
- what failed or created friction
- what actually worked
- what the reader should steal

### Posts to Avoid

- generic trend takes
- vague inspiration
- “5 tips” content with no lived experience
- product updates with no transferable lesson

---

## 12. Newsletter Strategy

The newsletter should be a utility product, not just a notification channel.

### Newsletter Promise

One useful workflow, one hard-earned lesson, and one resource worth stealing.

### What It Should Contain

- one tactical insight
- one current build note or failure
- one link to a new or updated site asset

### What It Should Not Become

- generic personal updates
- broad industry link roundups
- overly promotional broadcast copy

If the newsletter saves the reader time each issue, it will compound.

---

## 13. The Conversion Loops

People come back when the site creates loops, not just pages.

### Loop 1: Guide -> Template -> Subscribe

- reader lands on a guide
- guide links to a template or checklist
- template page invites subscription for future updates

### Loop 2: Project -> Build Log -> Guide

- reader sees an active project
- project links to recent build logs
- build log links to a deeper evergreen guide

### Loop 3: Field Note -> Comparison -> Resource Hub

- reader sees a fresh, current post
- links to a more durable comparison or guide
- eventually bookmarks the Resources page

### Loop 4: About -> Best Work -> Subscribe

- hiring manager or collaborator starts with About
- gets directed to best technical artifacts
- leaves with a clear sense of judgment and momentum

## 15. Editorial Rules

The site should adopt clear editorial standards.

### Rule 1: Specific beats broad

Prefer:

- one precise workflow
- one real failure
- one concrete comparison

over:

- a wide but shallow overview

### Rule 2: Artifact beats opinion

When possible, pair insight with:

- a checklist
- a prompt
- a template
- a screenshot
- a code example
- a decision framework

### Rule 3: Current work beats abstract commentary

If Joe is actively doing it, it is likely good material.

If Joe is only commenting on it from a distance, it is probably lower-value.

### Rule 4: Honest friction creates trust

Readers trust people who show:

- uncertainty
- debugging
- tradeoffs
- what did not work

### Rule 5: Every page needs a takeaway

A reader should leave with:

- a clearer decision
- a reusable idea
- a saved mistake
- a useful file or framework

## 16. Metrics That Actually Matter

Do not optimize for vanity traffic first.

The strongest early indicators are:

- newsletter subscriptions from content pages
- return visitors
- time on flagship resources
- clicks into related resources
- replies or direct outreach referencing specific pages
- pages that consistently attract navigation from other pages on the site

### Good Questions to Ask Monthly

- Which pages are earning repeat visits?
- Which pages are getting shared directly?
- Which pages convert readers into subscribers?
- Which topics produce the strongest follow-up conversations?
- Which pages feel like assets rather than one-off posts?

Timeframe:

- 1 to 2 weeks

Actions:

- finalize the site positioning
- decide on the flagship subject area
- define content categories
- rewrite homepage sections around utility and “start here” paths

### Phase 2: Build the Anchor Assets

Timeframe:

- 2 to 4 weeks

Actions:

- publish the Agentic Development Field Guide
- create the Resources page
- create at least 3 downloadable or copyable assets
- update the newsletter CTA to emphasize practical value

### Phase 3: Establish Publishing Loops

Timeframe:

- 4 to 8 weeks

Actions:

- launch a recurring field-notes series
- publish one comparison post
- publish one postmortem
- create stronger internal linking between posts, resources, and projects

### Phase 4: Tune Based on Real Behavior

Timeframe:

- ongoing

Actions:

- monitor what readers revisit
- expand the formats that earn repeat attention
- prune low-signal content ideas
- promote the pages that become true anchor assets

## 18. Recommended Priorities

These are the first priorities the site should take on.

1. Reposition the homepage around usefulness, not biography.
2. Create a flagship `Agentic Development Field Guide` page.
3. Add a `Resources` page with templates and checklists.
4. Start a recurring series with a rigid format.
5. Turn project pages into evidence that supports the writing, not isolated portfolio items.
6. Make the newsletter promise more tactical and specific.
7. Build a habit of publishing short field notes between deeper posts.
8. Treat every strong page as an asset that should keep compounding over time.

---

## 19. What to Say No To

To keep the site sharp, explicitly avoid:

- posting just to maintain frequency
- generic AI “thought leadership”
- motivational filler
- writing that sounds polished but teaches nothing
- overly broad topic coverage with no clear angle
- building too many disconnected sections before the flagship resources exist

Focus matters more than breadth.

---

## 20. Definition of Success

The site is succeeding when:

- engineers bookmark it for practical reference
- new readers immediately understand what it is for
- recurring series create anticipation
- one or two flagship resources become known entry points
- the newsletter grows because readers find it useful, not because the CTA is loud
- the body of work makes Joe look like a trustworthy operator, not just a competent marketer

That is the right direction.
